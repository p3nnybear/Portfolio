The Challenge

Time, Money, and Preference

The cloud storage market is still young and there is room for a particular audience. CloudShelf wants to provide specific features and services, particularly for interior designers and architects. I was delighted to help with design and development. Many interior designers and architects would need to send out files through email every day and it takes lots of time to pack the files. This issue can be solved if there is a cloud for them to use. Interior designers and architects do lots of markup and redlines every day at work in order to communicate design development. The markup feature added to the cloud will help to solve that problem. Concept images and material references are all over the place online. It’s hard to collect everything and organize them well. This issue can be solved if they can simply copy the link and upload on the cloud to organize them.

The Solution

CloudShelf allows the users to share all the files through the link, and provides the real-time markup service that solve the conflict on redlines. It allows users to collect and organize images, files, reference from other platforms.

The Discovery

Testing Waters

In order to know if there is a potential market for such prodcut, I needed to gather insights about the target market from both ends of the user. I posted one artist-specific survey to several DJs, artists, and musicians subreddit forums and one music-fans specific survey to several music discussion and discovery subreddit forums. I also sent the survey out to my friends who are acitively pursuing to become DJs. The survey questions received 35 responses from artists and musicians and 43 responses from music enthusiasts.

Market Confirmation

There is a consumer demand for Bömbox and a supply of artists who would charge a small fee or no fee at all. This is important because without the supply or the demand, the product would not survive.

Who are the users?

To gain a deeper understanding of my potential users, I interviewed them as they talked about their goals and frustrations. Not only did I confirm some of the survey results, I also discovered that this pool of audience heavily values user-friendly designs — to the point where they determine whether to keep using an app or not within the first few minutes of the product experience.

Throughout the project, I continously referred back to these personas to guide design decisions and prioroties and to ensure that I was always thinking from their shoes, addressing their goals and frustrations.

The Strategy

Information Architecture, User Stories, User Flows

With the product concept and personas in mind, I began constructing the mobile sitemap, which laid the foundation and structure of Bömbox. Followed by listing out all possible actions that make the MVP a truly user-friendly, time-saving, intuitive, and functional mobile app, I was able to prioritize and eliminate user stories while spotting issues in user flows and functionalities. For me, this part of the process reinforced the product concept, scopes, and goals of the app.

The Framework

Wireframe with Whimsical

Starting out with wireframes, I was able to explore all of my ideas. I didn’t have to worry about anything else but the structure and how content was going to be laid out. Using Whimsical, I worked on refining the hand-drawn sketch, focused on hierarchy, design elements, as well as placements. I created multiple layouts for the same screen and asked for feedback. Although paper wireframes can communicate a whole lot by themselves, creating a mid-fidelity wireframe helped me gather more insightful feedback from potential users. I believe it is important never to ask my users to imagine things because it can sometimes go in a very different direction.

In order to do some testings before jumping into high-fidelity mockups, I decided to create an interative prototype with Whimsical. I particularly tested the following flows

  1. Sign up/ Sign in process
  2. Adding file(s)
  3. Sharing file(s)

As a result, I needed to iterate, test, and achieve measureable outcomes to overcome these product shortcomings.

Conclusion

Lessons Learned

Don't sacrifice usability for aesthetics

I originally allowed users to use the app without signing up in my low/mid fidelity mockup but took it out in my first prototype for testing because I didn’t like the way it looked. Users immediately commented on not willing to input information until they knew what exactly they were signing up for. They helped me learn that sign up rate does not equate to conversion rate nor dollars made.

Spontaneous feedback gathered throughout the project = invaluable

I gathered feedback throughout the project and I learned that contanst feedback from users provides insights that could have been overlooked and helps my creative process.

Appreciate and comprehend the work of others

There were times when I felt stuck working on the project. Doing research and looking for inspirations has helped me broaden my creative library and practice thinking critically about each solution designers came up with solving their challenge.
